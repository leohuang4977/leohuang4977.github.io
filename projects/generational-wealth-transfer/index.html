Generational Wealth Transfer & Householding

How financial advisors actually manage complex, multi-generational client households, and where the tools they rely on keep getting in the way.

Role
Lead researcher
Method
25 advisor interviews, thematic synthesis
Scope
Multi-generational households
Output
Direction for relationship tooling
Portfolio version of real client work. Client and participant details are removed, and every figure is illustrative, drawn to show the method rather than report a client's results.
01

The brief

Trillions of dollars are moving from one generation to the next over the coming years, and a quiet retention problem sits underneath the transfer. When money transfers, most heirs leave the parents' advisor. A wealth-management platform wanted to help its advisors hold those relationships across generations. Doing that well meant the design team first needed a real picture of how advisors manage family households today, and why their current tools keep failing them.

That was the question I was brought in to answer before anyone drew a screen: what does it actually take to keep a family across a transfer, and what is the software doing to make that harder?

02

How I approached it

This was a depth question, not a counting one, so I ran it qualitatively. I interviewed twenty-five financial advisors in long, semi-structured sessions, recruited as a purposive sample that spanned firm type, tenure, and the complexity of the books they manage. The aim was range. I wanted the full spread of how households get handled, and I kept interviewing until the themes stopped shifting. Secondary research set the market context around those conversations.

When the interviews were done, I coded the transcripts thematically rather than reaching for the lines that happened to stick. The point of the study was to find the pattern advisors share, then turn it into something a product team could build against.

03

What I found

Three patterns came up over and over, across firms that otherwise had little in common.

FINDING 01Planning starts with a trigger, not a calendar

The first thing I heard, in almost every interview, was that transfer planning rarely starts on its own. Nobody wakes up and decides to map the family. The conversation opens when something happens in the client's life. A business sale. An inheritance. A health scare. A marriage or a divorce. A move into retirement. Advisors read those moments as the real prompts, and the good ones watch for them. Their software does nothing to help them see one coming.

FIG.02 — The moments that open the conversation

FINDING 02The household is a network. The CRM treats it as a list.

Every advisor I spoke to manages real families, which means several generations and roles that don't fit neat boxes. A matriarch who still controls the assets. An adult child who handles the logistics. An in-law nobody fully trusts. A trust with its own internal logic. The system they log into can't hold any of that. It models a primary contact and maybe a spouse, then stops. So advisors do what people always do when a tool won't bend. They build their own. Spreadsheets, side notes, and plain memory carry the household the CRM can't.

FIG.03 — The household the CRM can't hold

"My real household map is a spreadsheet I built myself. The CRM has no idea these ten people are one family."

FINDING 03Advisors already know the fix

What surprised me was how specific advisors were about the remedy. This wasn't a vague wish for better software. They named the same handful of things, firm after firm, whether they ran a small independent book or sat inside a large bank. That consistency is what turned a set of complaints into a clear product direction.

04

What advisors asked for

Flexible household mapping
Model real families: several generations, and roles that don't fit a box.
Cross-generational visibility
See the whole household's people and assets in one place.
Trigger surfacing
Flag the life events that should start a conversation.
Automated follow-up
Stop hand-tracking who needs what, and when.
Real integration
End the double-entry into private spreadsheets the system never sees.
One household, one view
A record that stays whole as the family, and the assets, move down a generation.
FIG.04 — From complaints to a product direction

The same needs recurred across very different firms, which is what made the direction defensible rather than anecdotal.

05

Why heirs leave, and what keeps them

What loses the next generation

An advisor the heirs never actually met. A transfer handled like paperwork. And a system that kept the wider family invisible until the moment had already passed.

What keeps them

Real relationships built across the household early. The advisor seen as the family's advisor, rather than only the parents'. Tooling that helps them show up at the right moment instead of fighting them for it.

06

My role

I led the qualitative side of this study. I shaped the research questions with the product and design partners, wrote the interview guide, and ran the interviews and the analysis. The harder part was synthesis: turning a stack of long transcripts into a small set of themes a product team could build against, and doing it in a way that held up when people pushed on it. I stayed close to the design and product partners through that synthesis, so the findings landed as shared decisions instead of a deck that gets read once and filed.
