Generational Wealth Transfer & Householding

A qualitative study of how advisors manage complex, multi-generational client households — and where their tools fall short

A note on the data. This is an anonymized, portfolio version of qualitative client work. Participant details are disguised, the advisor quotes are illustrative composites, and the framing figures come from secondary research rather than this study.

The brief

An enormous amount of wealth is moving from one generation to the next — and the industry has a retention problem: when assets transfer, most heirs leave the parents’ advisor. A wealth-management platform wanted to help its advisors keep those relationships across generations. Before designing anything, we needed to understand how advisors actually manage complex family households today, and why their existing tools keep getting in the way.

How I approached it

This was a depth question, not a counting question, so I ran it qualitatively. I conducted twelve in-depth interviews with financial advisors, recruited as a purposive sample spanning firm type, tenure, and book complexity — the goal being to hear the full range of how households are managed, and to keep interviewing until themes stopped changing. Secondary research framed the market around those conversations. I then coded the transcripts thematically rather than cherry-picking memorable lines.

The moments that open the conversation

The first thing I heard across interviews: transfer planning almost never starts on its own. It’s triggered by life events — and by the time the event arrives, it’s too late to start building the relationship. Advisors who retain the next generation are already in the household when these moments hit.

What I heard

1. Transfer is relationship-led, and the advisor has to start it

The advisors who keep heirs treat the whole family as the client years before any money moves — meeting the kids, sitting in on conversations, becoming “the family’s advisor” rather than “Mom and Dad’s advisor.” The ones who lose heirs wait for the transfer and then try to earn a relationship that no longer has time to form.

“By the time the inheritance is happening, the decision’s already made. If I haven’t known the kids for years, I’m a stranger asking to manage their money.” — P7, family office advisor (illustrative)

2. The tools model individuals; wealth lives in households

This was the core product insight. CRMs are built around individual contacts and accounts in a flat list. Real wealth lives in an interconnected household — couples, children, grandchildren, and the non-linear professional roles (CPAs, attorneys, trustees) woven around them. Because the software can’t represent that, advisors fall back on spreadsheets, notes, and memory to track who’s who.

“My real household map is a spreadsheet I built myself. The CRM has no idea these ten people are one family.” — P3, private banker (illustrative)

3. Advisors know exactly what they’re missing

Far from being vague, advisors were specific about what would help — and consistent about it across very different firms.

What advisors want

  • Flexible household mapping. Model real families — multiple generations and non-linear roles — not just primary-and-spouse.
  • Cross-generational visibility. See the whole household's relationships and assets in one place.
  • Automation of routine follow-up. Stop manually tracking who needs what and when.
  • Surfacing of transfer triggers. Flag the life events that should prompt a conversation.
  • Real integration. End the double-entry into private spreadsheets that the system can't see.

Why heirs leave — and what keeps them

What loses the next generation

An advisor who only ever knew the parents, a transfer handled as a transaction, and tooling that never made the wider family visible until it was too late to engage them.

What keeps them

Early, genuine relationships across the household; being seen as the family's advisor; and systems that help the advisor show up at the right moments instead of getting in the way.

On synthesis, and what I’d validate next

Qualitative work earns its credibility from how it’s handled, so I try to be explicit about mine:

  • Twelve interviews answer “how” and “why,” not “how many.” I sampled for range and interviewed to saturation, which is the right basis for themes — and the wrong basis for prevalence. That’s why I don’t report percentages from these conversations; the only numbers here are secondary-research context.
  • Secondary sources are framing, not ground truth. Industry figures carry their own selection and recency biases, so I triangulated across sources and used them to size the stakes, not to settle questions.
  • A vivid quote isn’t a finding. I coded across all twelve transcripts and weighted what recurred, so the themes reflect the pattern rather than the most quotable advisor.
  • The next steps are about magnitude and proof. I’d run a quantitative advisor survey to size these themes, concept- and usability-test a relationship-mapping prototype with advisors, and — ideally — track whether better householding actually improves heir retention, since that’s the business outcome the whole effort exists to move.

What I want this study to show isn’t just that I can run interviews — it’s that I can turn twelve conversations into a defensible product direction while being precise about the line between insight and evidence.
