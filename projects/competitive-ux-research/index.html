Competitive Benchmarking · Mixed-Method Usability

Where a wealth-management site stood against its rivals: what to fix first.

A within-subjects competitive benchmark and a deep-dive page evaluation, both run with high-net-worth users, turned a vague "the site underperforms" into a prioritized, evidence-backed brief for the redesign.

// Anonymized account of confidential client work. The client and its competitors are disguised, and the charts are illustrative recreations made for this portfolio.
01

The challenge

A wealth-management firm's public website was underperforming as a front door for new clients. Before committing budget to a rebuild, the team needed two answers: how the site compared to the competition, and where its own pages were losing people. I was brought in to settle both with evidence rather than opinion.

02

My role & the team

I worked on this inside a small revamp pod with a principal UX researcher and a designer. The other researcher and I shaped the questions, designed both studies, and screened a hard-to-reach audience. From there I ran the sessions, analyzed the quantitative and qualitative data, built it into a prioritized set of recommendations, and presented those to the client.

We worked closely with the designer the whole way through, so every redesign decision made sense and looked right on the page. Once the direction was set, it went to the developer, who built it.

03

How I approached it

I ran two complementary studies so the team could see both the outside-in and the inside-out picture.

Study 1 — Competitive

Within-subjects benchmark

~30 high-net-worth participants (screened on investable assets and personal investing experience) completed the same tasks and rating scales across the client's site and several leading competitors. Within-subjects made each participant their own benchmark, which keeps the comparisons clean.

Study 2 — Comprehensive

Deep-dive page evaluation

~30 participants went deep on the client's own key pages (home, solutions, and audience pages), looking at how well each communicated its purpose and exactly where comprehension broke down.

Both blended task-based usability metrics, Likert ratings of clarity, visual appeal, and likelihood to consider, and open-ended responses I coded thematically. I leaned on statistical testing to separate real differences from noise rather than over-reading a small sample.

What the research surfaced

Where the site landed in the field

I combined first-choice preference with full ordinal rankings to place the site within its competitive set, then tested which gaps were statistically meaningful and which fell within the margin of a 30-person study.

Relative standing, illustrative. No scale implied. Ties that fell within the margin of a 30-person study were reported as ties, not over-read.
How it scored on the dimensions that mattered

The gap wasn't uniform. The site held its own on some attributes and trailed on others, which is exactly the nuance that tells a design team where to spend effort.

FIG.03 — Performance by dimension
Illustrative. Positions are relative, not scored values. At par on clarity and look, with clear room to close on trust, detail, and differentiation.
What people actually said

The numbers tell you that something is off. The words tell you why. I coded several hundred open-ended comments into recurring themes, turning a wall of free text into a short, prioritized list of what was working and what to fix.

Theme — what held up

Credible, clean, navigable

The brand read as established, the layout felt uncluttered, and people mostly found their way. Comments here were short. Nothing to fix is a finding too.

Theme — what broke

Vague services, thin proof

The longest comment threads asked for specifics: what the firm actually does, what it costs, and why anyone should believe the claims. People wanted evidence, not adjectives.

05

From insight to direction

The research converged on a clear, prioritized direction. Every recommendation traced to a specific pattern in the data, so the team could weigh effort against evidence instead of opinion.

01

Deepen service clarity

Give pages the detail and specificity the research showed was missing. It was the top driver of where comprehension broke down.

02

Make trust concrete

Add the credibility signals clients said they look for, rather than asking them to take the brand on faith.

03

Show, don't tell

A more interactive, metric-forward experience that proves the offer instead of describing it in generic copy.

04

Sharpen differentiation

A crisp point of distinction in a field where the sites, including the client's, largely read alike.

FIG.04 — Direction
Illustrative contrast, not the client's UI. The research pointed away from the dense, generic pattern (left) toward a cleaner, trust-forward direction (right).
06

What it changed

We turned the prioritized findings into an evidence-backed redesign, built out in Figma. The client approved that direction and had it implemented within the quarter. Over the following year, the channel converted roughly five times as many visitors into customers.
