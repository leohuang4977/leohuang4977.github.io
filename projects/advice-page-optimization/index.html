Survey Research · Ranking & Likert · Wealth Management

What moves someone to contact an advisor, and what just gets in the way.

A 150-person survey of current and prospective wealth clients, built to answer three questions before a redesign: what people value in an advisor, what moves them to reach out, and how they want to do it.

01

The brief

A wealth-management firm was redesigning the page where prospective clients learn about its advice offering and decide whether to get in touch. Before locking the design, the team wanted answers from clients themselves: what they actually value in an advisor, what would prompt them to make contact, and how they would want to do it. I built one survey to answer all three at once.

02

My role & the team

I ran this study end to end inside the bank's research practice: scoping the questions with the partners who owned the advice and contact experience, writing the 16-item instrument, setting the screening criteria, and fielding the survey. The analysis was mine as well, both the segmentation cuts and the thematic coding of the open-ended responses. The readout went back to those partners as design direction rather than a data dump.

03

How I ran it

I designed a 16-item instrument that mixed three kinds of questions. Ranking tasks forced trade-offs instead of letting everything be important. Likert items measured strength of feeling on the things the rankings surfaced. Open-ended questions caught whatever the structured items missed.

I fielded it to 150 current and prospective clients, screened on investing experience and investable assets so the sample looked like the audience the page serves. Then I read the results two ways: segmentation cuts by age, region, and investor type, and thematic coding across every open-ended response.

04

What clients value

The rankings were not close. Trustworthiness, ethical standards, and deep experience sat far above everything else. Capability claims, the technology and the global reach the industry likes to advertise, clustered at the bottom of the list in every segment we cut.

Valuing an advisor and contacting one are different decisions, so the instrument asked about them separately rather than letting one stand in for the other. The credibility result held on both sides. It is what people respect, and it is what gets them to reach out.

FIG.02 — What clients value in an advisor
Ranked importance, all segments pooled. Conceptual, with illustrative lengths. The gap between the credibility cluster and the capability cluster was the study's clearest result.
05

How they want to reach out

When clients decide to make contact, they want a person. Phone, email, and in-person conversations led in every segment. The web form came last, and the open-ended comments explained why: a form feels like a message into the void when the whole point is to start a relationship.

FIG.03 — Preferred first contact
First-choice channel for starting a conversation. Conceptual, with illustrative lengths.

The form itself produced the study's most useful nuance. There was no consensus on how long it should be. There was near-total agreement on what belongs in it. People volunteered the same three things: why they are reaching out, what they want help with, and enough about their financial situation to make the first conversation useful. The fix was not a shorter form. It was the right fields.

FIG.04 — The form
No consensus on form length. Near-total agreement on which fields matter. The highlighted rows are the ones clients volunteered unprompted.
06

From insight to design

The readout went to the team as three design directions, each tied to the evidence behind it.

REC 01

Lead with credibility

Put trustworthiness, ethics, and experience at the top of the page, in plain language with proof behind it. Capability messaging can support, but it should not lead.

REC 02

Streamline the form

Ask for what clients already want to share: why they are reaching out, what they want help with, and enough financial context to route them well. Every field beyond that is friction with no consensus behind it.

REC 03

Offer real channels

Make a phone number, an email address, and a way to book a conversation as prominent as the form. The form is the fallback. The page should not treat it as the main event.
