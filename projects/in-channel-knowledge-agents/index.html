Mixed-Method · Survey + Behavioral Telemetry · Team Chat

What Users Want from In-Channel Knowledge Agents

Where an AI help-agent earns its place inside team chat, settled by reading what people say about getting help against what they actually do to get it.

Portfolio version. A generalized, anonymized account of real research. The product is unnamed and every figure is illustrative, recreated to show the method and the reasoning rather than actual results.

MethodMixed-method, triangulated
LensesSurvey + behavioral telemetry
SampleN = 412 survey · 6-week telemetry window
01

The brief

A team-chat platform was weighing an in-channel knowledge agent: an AI that answers questions where people already work, instead of sending them off to a separate knowledge base. Three things had to be settled before any of it was worth building. Whether people wanted it at all. Where it would earn its place. How it should behave once it got there.

I ran the study mixed-method on purpose, because what people say about getting help and what they do to get it are rarely the same.

02

How I ran it

I used two lenses and read them against each other. A survey captured attitudes, demand, and what people believe they do. Behavioral telemetry captured what they actually do in-channel.

The triangulation is the whole design. Each lens covers the other's blind spot, and the findings that matter most sit exactly where the two disagree.

03

Big appetite, leaky adoption

The demand signal was strong but lossy. Most people want in-channel help. Fewer can get it today, and fewer still use what already exists. Every step from wanting help to actually using it sheds people, and that leak is the gap an agent would have to close.

04

What people say versus what they do

This is the finding the mixed-method design was built to catch. On the survey, people described themselves as searching the knowledge base first. In the telemetry, they overwhelmingly direct-messaged a person instead.

The help is real and generous. It just happens in private DMs, where no one else can see it and it never becomes reusable. The knowledge base stays quiet not because people refuse to help, but because helping privately is faster.

That one disagreement reframed the product. The job is not to push people toward search. It is to surface what is already locked inside DMs and threads, and turn one-off answers into something the whole team can find later.

05

What users want the agent to do

The headline wants all point the same direction: surface what already exists. People asked for past answers brought back up, confident replies to the questions that come up over and over, and quick summaries of long threads.

Graceful handoff ranked lower in raw demand, but it is what makes everything above it safe to trust. Suggesting the right expert, or flagging a human the moment the agent is unsure, is a guardrail rather than a feature.

06

Where help happens, and where the agent belongs

The second disagreement was about scope, and it produced a clean crossover. Most peer help today lands on live project work, which is the judgment-heavy territory where people are least willing to hand things to an agent. Where they do want an agent is the knowable, low-judgment material: policy and HR, IT and tools, how-to and process.

An agent that respects that line earns trust. One that wades into live project judgment loses it.

07

What this means for design

Scope

Scope to the knowable

Point the agent at policy, IT, and how-to. Leave the live project judgment that people want to keep human.

Surface

Surface buried answers

Mine threads, and the patterns behind what people DM about, then turn them into answers the team can actually find.

Trust

Hand off gracefully

Answer only when confident. When it is not, name the right person or loop in a human instead of guessing.

Place

Meet people in-channel

Put help where the conversation already is, so reuse never depends on opening a separate tool.

08

What it changed

The recommendation shipped. The agent went out as a scoped pilot in the knowable domains the study marked out, with live project judgment left to people. Satisfaction with the pilot came back strong, which suggested the scope line was drawn about right.

09

My role and the team

I led the research, working with a product manager and a designer who helped scope the questions and carried the findings into the product. Data science partnered on the telemetry side, pulling the in-channel logs the behavioral half of the study ran on. The survey, the analysis, and the triangulation between the two were mine.

10

What I'd validate next

What I want this study to demonstrate is mixed-method judgment: knowing what each lens can and cannot tell you, and designing for the seam between them. Part of that judgment is being clear about what the study could not settle.

  • Telemetry shows what, not why. A DM might be relationship-building rather than search-avoidance. I would pair the behavioral data with a handful of contextual interviews so I am not reading intent into clicks.
  • The two samples only overlap. Survey respondents and the telemetry population were not identical, so I treat the comparison as directional and would reconcile it at the individual level wherever the data allows.
  • The pilot is the next data source. Now that the agent is live in the knowable domains, the numbers worth tracking are whether it converts buried DMs into reusable answers, and how often it answers confidently when it should have handed off. A wrong answer delivered with confidence is the fastest way to spend the trust the scoping earned.
