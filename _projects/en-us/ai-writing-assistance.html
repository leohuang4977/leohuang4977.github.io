What people want from a writing assistant, and how high they set the bar.

A weighted survey measuring two things at once: how much people want an in-product writing assistant, and the quality it has to clear before they keep using it. The demand was easy to find. The bar was the harder number, and the one that decided the build.

// Anonymized account of client work. The numbers on this page, figures and outcome metrics included, are illustrative reconstructions for portfolio use. No client names, real estimates, or proprietary findings appear. The methodology is the study's real architecture: the weighting, the confidence intervals, and the segmentation all ran as described.
01

The question

A product team was deciding whether to add an AI writing assistant to a piece of workplace software, and how good it had to be on day one. "Do people want this" is the easy version of that question. The one that shapes a roadmap is quieter: how good does the first version have to be before people keep it on instead of switching it off.

A weak assistant does not sit harmlessly at zero. It costs trust, and people stop opening the feature after a couple of bad suggestions. So the study measured demand and the adoption threshold together, because shipping below the bar is worse than not shipping at all.

02

Approach

This was a weighted survey of 1,000 people who write as part of their job, built to give estimates I could stand behind rather than a read on whoever happened to answer. A raw survey sample leans toward the people most willing to fill one out, so responses were weighted to workforce demographics before any number got reported.

Every estimate carries a 95% confidence interval. A four-point gap between two options means something different when the intervals overlap than when they sit clearly apart, and reporting the intervals kept that distinction in front of the team. Results were cut by role, tenure, and current AI use, since a daily AI user and a skeptic want different things from the same feature.

03

What we found

Three findings carried the recommendation. They build on each other, so the order matters.

The demand question came back loud. Across roles and tenure, most people said they wanted writing help, and the segments agreed with each other more than expected. The threshold question came back louder. When we asked how good the feature had to be before they would leave it on, the bar people set sat well above what a fast first version tends to deliver. FIG.01 puts both on the same axis.

FIG.01 — Where the adoption bar sits
Demand sat high across segments. The quality people required before they would keep the feature sat higher. A version good enough to demo can still land inside the zone where people turn it off.

That gap between a quick MVP and the adoption bar is the whole argument of the study. It is also why the analysis leaned so hard on precision. If the team was going to gate a launch on a threshold, the estimates feeding that call had to hold up, so every number went out with a 95% confidence interval. FIG.02 shows how that played out across the four launch criteria the team cared about most.

FIG.02 — Weighted estimates, 95% CI
Reporting every estimate with its interval kept the team from chasing differences that were inside the noise. Two priorities cleared the line cleanly. Two overlapped it, and got held back from the roadmap.

Reading FIG.02 takes ten seconds once you know the trick. A dot is the weighted estimate, the whiskers are the range the data can actually support, and the dashed line is the decision threshold. Where the whole interval sits past the line, the finding holds even at the pessimistic end. Where the interval crosses it, the honest answer is "can't tell yet," and accuracy and tone match were the two that held. That left one question: of everything people said mattered, what actually moves them to adopt? FIG.03 ranks the answers.

FIG.03 — What earns adoption
When people described what would make them keep the feature, a short list did most of the work, and accuracy led by a wide margin. Volume of suggestions ranked low. People did not want more help. They wanted help they could trust without re-reading.
04

What it changed

The recommendation was to hold the launch to a high accuracy bar and cut scope rather than ship a broad, average version. The development team used the findings to focus their quality work, putting effort against accuracy and tone match and trimming what had ranked low.

The product shipped on that narrower scope and was well received. In a later in-product review survey, satisfaction averaged 4.2 out of 5, and weekly usage settled around 31% of eligible users by the end of the first quarter. For an optional assist feature, that is the pattern you want: people who tried it kept it on.

05

My role

I was the lead UX researcher on the study, working with the product manager and the designer. The PM brought the roadmap questions, and part of my job was to guide and narrow them into things a survey can measure cleanly. From there I designed the instrument, set the weighting scheme, and ran the analysis in Python. The PM and I presented the readout together, pairing what the data said with what the team should do about it.
