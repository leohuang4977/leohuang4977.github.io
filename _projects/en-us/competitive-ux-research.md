> **A note on this case study.** This write-up generalizes confidential client work. The client and its competitors are anonymized, and **every figure and number on this page is illustrative, recreated for this portfolio** — not the original study's results. It's here to show how I approach research, not to share any client's findings.

## The challenge

A wealth-management firm's public website was underperforming as a front door for new clients. Before committing to a redesign, the team needed to answer two questions: how does the site compare to the competition, and where are its own pages losing people? I was brought in to find out.

## My role

I owned the research end to end — shaping the questions, designing both studies, defining and screening the target audience, running the sessions, analyzing the quantitative and qualitative data, and turning the results into a prioritized set of recommendations the product and design teams could act on.

## Approach

I ran two complementary studies so the team could see both the outside-in and the inside-out picture.

**Competitive usability test.** A within-subjects study with ~15 high-net-worth participants (screened on investable assets and personal investing experience) who completed the same tasks and rating scales across the client's site and several leading competitors. Running it within-subjects made each participant their own benchmark, which keeps the comparisons clean even with a modest sample.

**Comprehensive usability test.** A focused study with ~10 participants that went deep on the client's own key pages — home, solutions, and audience pages — examining how well each communicated its purpose and where comprehension broke down.

Both blended task-based usability metrics, Likert ratings of clarity, visual appeal, and likelihood to consider, and open-ended responses that I coded thematically. I leaned on statistical testing to separate real differences from noise rather than over-reading a small sample.

## What the research surfaced

### Where the site landed in the field

{% include figure.liquid loading="eager" path="assets/img/wealth-ux-ranking.png" class="img-fluid rounded z-depth-1" zoomable=true %}

I combined first-choice preference with full ordinal rankings to place the site within its competitive set, then tested which gaps were statistically meaningful and which fell within the margin of a 15-person study.

### How it scored on the dimensions that mattered

{% include figure.liquid loading="eager" path="assets/img/wealth-ux-dimensions.png" class="img-fluid rounded z-depth-1" zoomable=true %}

Breaking the ratings out by dimension showed the gap wasn't uniform: the site held its own on some attributes while trailing on others — exactly the nuance that tells a design team where to spend effort.

### What people actually said

{% include figure.liquid loading="eager" path="assets/img/wealth-ux-themes.png" class="img-fluid rounded z-depth-1" zoomable=true %}

The numbers tell you *that* something is off; the words tell you *why*. I coded several hundred open-ended comments into recurring themes, turning a wall of free text into a short, prioritized list of what was working and what to fix.

## From insight to direction

The research converged on a clear, prioritized direction for the redesign: deepen the clarity and detail of service information, add concrete trust signals, make the experience more interactive and metric-forward, and give the brand a sharper point of differentiation. Each recommendation traced back to a specific pattern in the data, so the team could weigh effort against evidence instead of opinion.

## What this project demonstrates

- Designing rigorous mixed-methods research — within-subjects competitive benchmarking paired with in-depth page evaluation
- Recruiting and working with a hard-to-reach, high-net-worth audience
- Pairing quantitative usability metrics with thematic analysis of qualitative data
- Using statistical testing to keep conclusions honest at a realistic sample size
- Translating ambiguous signal into a prioritized, build-ready design direction
