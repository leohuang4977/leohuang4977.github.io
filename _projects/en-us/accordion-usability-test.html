Open vs. Closed: an accordion navigation test

A moderated usability test of two in-app navigation defaults. Stated preference couldn't settle it. Behavior could.

01

The brief

In the mobile app, a screen's sections collapse into an accordion. That left the design team with a small but real choice: should the first section open the moment the screen loads, or should everything start collapsed? They wanted to know which default got people to content faster, and whether anyone even noticed the difference.

02

My role

The product designer owned the navigation pattern and built both prototypes in Figma. I was in those design sessions with him, working through the choices side by side, and I owned the research end of it: I shaped the question and the tasks, moderated every session, and analyzed the timing. He had a strong hunch that open-by-default felt friendlier. I wanted the call made on behavior, not instinct, so we turned the hunch into a test. We watched the session clips together afterward, which meant the decision came out of evidence we both saw rather than one person's taste.

03

What I compared

The two prototypes differed in exactly one thing: whether the first section started open or collapsed. We ran it within-subjects, so every participant used both versions and did the same find-the-content tasks on each. I tracked two things in parallel: which version people said they preferred, and how long they actually took to reach content in each.

FIG.01 — The one difference
Identical prototypes apart from one toggle: the opening state of the first section. Conceptual rendering.
04

No clear favorite

Asked which version they preferred, people split, and about half had no strong opinion either way. A useful non-result. Preference couldn't decide this, so the behavioral data would carry the call.

FIG.02 — Stated preference
Across 20 people, no default won on opinion: half had no strong preference and the rest split evenly. Illustrative.
05

Open is faster, especially deeper in the screen

Behavior told a clearer story than opinion did. People reached content faster when the first section was already open, and the advantage grew the further down the screen the target sat. Starting collapsed taxes every tap. Starting open pays that back, and it pays the most on the longest screens.

FIG.03 — Time to reach content, by screen depth
Both defaults slow down deeper in the screen, but starting collapsed slows faster, so the gap is widest at the bottom. Illustrative shape, not measured values.
06

The call

Recommendation 01

Default to the first section open

It measurably speeds up reaching content at no cost in stated preference. A low-risk default the team could ship with confidence.

Recommendation 02

Honor the deep link

When someone opens the app from a deep link, open the section they came for instead of always the first. Let intent override the default.

FIG.04 — The shipping rule
Open-first as the baseline, with a deep link allowed to override it. Conceptual.
07

What it changed

The design team adopted the open-first direction for the redesigned section screens, and the deep-link rule shipped with it, so an incoming link now lands people on the section they actually wanted. The timing result is what moved the conversation from opinion to a decision the team could commit to.

I scoped a larger unmoderated timing test as the follow-up to size the effect before the team leaned on it any harder.

Small, real, and settled by watching what people do. My favorite kind of question.
