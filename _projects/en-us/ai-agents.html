Strategy & Segmentation · Large-Scale Weighted Survey

How ready people were for AI agents at work, and where to start.

A large stratified survey, weighted to stand in for the whole user base, that turned an open-ended bet on AI agents into a defensible order of operations.

// Anonymized account of confidential client work. The platform is unnamed and the charts are illustrative recreations made for this portfolio.
01

The brief

A platform was weighing how seriously to invest in AI agents, the kind that take actions for a user rather than only answering questions. The leadership question was strategic rather than technical. Were customers ready, where should a first release land, and what would hold adoption back? The research had to turn an open-ended bet into a sequenced plan.

Are they ready?

How aware and how open the user base is, and how far that sits from real usage today.

Where do we start?

Which teams and which agent types make the natural first release.

What's in the way?

The concerns most likely to stall adoption, and what they mean for design.

02

My role & the team

I worked on this study as part of a research team with a VP of Research and a senior UX researcher. The senior researcher and I did most of the hands-on work, in close collaboration. We shaped the questions, designed and weighted the survey, ran the analysis, and presented the results to leadership.

03

How I ran it

I ran a large stratified survey and applied two-stage weighting so the results would represent the whole user base, not the subset who happened to respond. Post-stratification matched the responses to the known population, and a non-response adjustment brought them closer to the people who did not reply. That is what lets the findings generalize.

The companion writing-assistant study goes deeper on the statistics. Here the method is the foundation, and the story is what it told the team to do.

04

What the research surfaced

High awareness, low usage

The first finding framed everything else. Almost everyone knew about agents, but very few had used one for real work, a gap of roughly four to one. That distance is the size of the opportunity: a well-aimed first release could move a lot of people at once.

FIG.02 — From aware to actually using
Awareness is near-universal and openness stays high. The drop happens at real, hands-on use, which is the gap a first release would need to close.
Openness is high, and mostly untapped

The gap was not driven by resistance. Asked how open they were to using agents inside the platform they already work in, about six in ten leaned positive and only about two in ten were opposed. The audience was willing. It just had not been handed the right first thing to try. Openness is not the same as adoption, though, so I treated it as a reason to pilot rather than a forecast to bank on.

Where to start: the beachhead

This is the part that changed the roadmap conversation. Readiness was not uniform. It clustered in specific job functions, and each high-readiness function wanted a different first agent. Cutting the data this way turns "build agents" into a concrete sequence: start where awareness and openness are highest, and lead with the capability that function actually asked for.

These function-level cuts are directional, since the sub-samples shrink once you segment and the margins widen. I would confirm the beachhead with targeted follow-up before committing the roadmap to it.

FIG.03 — The beachhead
Illustrative. Readiness clusters by function, and each one wants a different first agent. The two highest-readiness functions are the natural place to start.
The barrier that moved to the front

Among the people who were hesitant, the top concern had shifted. In earlier waves of AI research, accuracy and reliability led. Here, ethical implications ranked first. That turns mitigation into a design problem rather than a messaging one. Transparency, control, and a visible human in the loop become requirements in the product, not reassurances bolted on at the end.

FIG.04 — The barrier shifted
Illustrative. The top concern moved from accuracy to ethics, which is why transparency and human oversight belong in the design, not the launch messaging.
How they want to adopt

The shape of demand was as telling as its size. People did not want agents acting on them in the background. They wanted to stay in control of when help showed up.

Invoked, not ambient

A clear preference for agents you call on over always-on automation.

Private first

A private space to work with an agent before anything becomes shared or visible.

Human in the loop

Agents that propose and assist, with the person keeping the final call.

05

From findings to a roadmap

The findings pointed to a small set of choices the team could defend.

01

Sequence by readiness

Start in the beachhead functions and ship each one the agent it actually asked for, so the early wins land where adoption is likeliest.

02

Build for invocation and privacy

Because that is how people said they want to adopt, the interaction model and the privacy model are first-release requirements, not later polish.

03

Treat ethics as a core surface

Since it is now the top barrier, transparency and human oversight belong in the core design rather than a trust-and-safety appendix.

06

What it changed

The study turned into a plan the team acted on. It anchored a roadmap shareout for the next year, and two of the highest-readiness functions got a working pilot. Each one shipped the agent that function had asked for, scoped to a specific subsample so the results stayed clean. In those groups the agents earned real, measured use where the survey had found awareness without usage. The support lookup agent handled close to half of routine questions on its own and cut time-to-answer by about a third, and across both pilots roughly four in five users said they wanted to keep it.
