Why customers switch mortgage providers

A two-group survey built to read switchers and non-switchers side by side, so a bank could see what actually moves a mortgage and what keeps one parked.

Anonymized portfolio version of a client study. The bank is unnamed and the figures are illustrative, recreated to show the design and analysis rather than to report real results.
By the numbers
Participants200
Two balanced groups100 switchers / 100 stayers
Survey items16
DesignComparative, two-group
SectorRetail banking
01

The brief

A retail bank wanted to win more of the mortgages its customers held elsewhere, and a redesign of the switching flow was on the table. Before anyone touched the page, the open question was behavioral. What actually moves someone to bring a mortgage over, and what keeps an otherwise eligible customer where they are?

02

My role & the team

I was the researcher on the squad redesigning the switching flow, working day to day with the lead designer and a product manager, with a senior researcher reviewing the work. I wrote the 16-item questionnaire, including the true/false knowledge check, and set the screening rules that split recruitment into balanced groups of recent switchers and stayers. Once the data was in I ran the analysis end to end, then brought the findings into the squad's design reviews, where the three recommendations below took shape.

03

How I ran it

The design was built for comparison. I recruited a balanced sample, 100 recent switchers and 100 people who had stayed with their provider, so the two groups could be read against each other. Everyone answered the same set of questions: a few demographics, a short true/false check on how mortgages work, a ranking of what matters when choosing a provider, and how hard they expected switching to be. From there I compared the groups item by item, tested which gaps held up statistically, and looked for places where two factors worked together rather than reading each one on its own.

04

What we found

Experience predicts switching

The strongest signal had little to do with who someone was and a lot to do with what they had already done. People who had switched a mortgage before stood well apart from those who never had, on intent, on confidence, and on how easy they expected the move to be. Age, income, and region barely registered next to that.

Difficulty is the wall for first-timers

Expected difficulty mattered, but mostly for one group. For people who had never switched, the harder the process looked, the more likely they were to stay put. For people who had done it before, difficulty hardly moved them. The two groups only met at the point where switching felt genuinely easy. The same first-timers scored lowest on the true/false knowledge check, so the dread tracks what people have not done yet more than what the process actually demands.

Rate sits on top

One factor outranked the rest from every angle we asked about. Why people would switch, what they weighed most, what put them off: the interest rate came first each time. Fees, trust, and the existing relationship all sat well behind it.

05

From insight to design

01

Demystify the move

Show, step by step, how ordinary switching a mortgage really is. Aim it at first-time switchers, who tend to overestimate the hassle.

02

Put the rate first

Make the rate the most prominent, scannable thing on the page. It drives the decision more than anything else does.

03

Clear the obstacles early

Name the things people dread, like paperwork and penalties, and answer them up front instead of leaving them to the end of the flow.

06

What it changed

The study fed a redesign of the switching flow, and the team shipped it. The new version followed the recommendations above, with the rate pulled to the top of the page and the process laid out step by step for people who had never moved a mortgage before. Switching volume picked up in the months that followed. I hold that result loosely, though. Rates were moving over the same stretch, and without a holdout there is no clean way to split what the redesign earned from what the market handed us.

METHODS & TOOLS
Comparative two-group survey · balanced screener recruitment · ranked-factor & Likert measurement · group-difference & interaction testing · Qualtrics · Python
